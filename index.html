The Comments Section: How Manufactured Consensus Undermines Cognitive Autonomy

May 19, 2026

Recently, I've been bombarded with YouTube Shorts of an interview with Theo Von and Ella Langley. After watching each clip, I inevitably check the comments section and it immediately becomes clear that the internet "ships" these two. Everyone in the comments section seems to agree that they have a romantic connection, a spiritual connection, and should get together. Why? I don't know. Perhaps it's obvious and I'm oblivious. But as I watch more and more shorts of this interview and subsequently read more and more comments, I feel like I'm in on the bit. Like I'm a guest at a proposal party nervously waiting for the potential bride to show up. I find myself grinning when I catch a brief moment where I believe they're flirting.

As adorable as that is - I don't actually think they'd be a cute couple. I don't know them! He's 20 years older than her! I also don't actually believe that they're flirting, it just seems like two people having fun at an interview. Yet, subliminally, I now do in fact think they would make a cute couple.

Somewhere along the way I stopped merely observing the consensus and started participating in it. The scale of agreement led me to believe that it's obvious they belong together. Surely, hundreds of people cannot all be wrong. But, not everyone who has commented, every viewer, or even a majority of either group, believes they would be a cute couple. The comments section, curated to show the comments with the most "likes," just leads me to believe that. This exemplifies a phenomenon I call "manufactured consensus."

Consequently, I subconsciously accepted that Theo and Ella would be a cute couple. My cognitive autonomy has been undermined. Cognitive autonomy is our ability to encounter information, critically evaluate, and arrive at a conclusion. But, while surfing social media, we check the comments section and encounter consensus first. In doing so, we outsource our thinking and critically impair our ability to reach a conscious, intentional conclusion. This is what it means to have our cognitive autonomy undermined.

In real life, we don't call it manufactured consensus. We call it peer pressure. Even the most confident, self-assured individuals among us are guilty of succumbing to peer pressure. Sometimes, the weight of the collective is so overwhelming we don't have time to fully understand our position on the matter before we've abandoned even trying. Sometimes, knowingly succumbing is just the path of least resistance. These moments and decisions happen organically on a regular basis. Especially as youths.

Consider a group of 6 teens who go out drinking. One proposes subway surfing (riding atop and traversing moving subways). Five agree; one hesitates. The group uses different strategies – shared vulnerability, hasty generalizations, dubious appeals to authority – but convinces as a whole. As our skeptic is faced with each argument, his confidence in his own stance slowly erodes. Each individual argument may crumble when scrutinized but combined, it's difficult to disagree. Without peer pressure, perhaps our skeptic would choose differently. But in that moment, he subway-surfs.

The peer-pressure hypothetical mirrors manufactured consensus with some key differences. In the real world, you're regularly exposed to consequences of your actions. If one of our young skeptic's friends falls and injures himself while subway surfing, our skeptic receives meaningful feedback that contextualizes the dangers of subway surfing. Continued exposure to the same echo chamber allows you to see how people's arguments are full of holes. Technologically, there is no feedback loop. Ideas are absorbed and left to calcify, with nothing in your environment to test or challenge them.

With peer pressure, you know you're being pressured. You're acutely aware of the adversarial nature of the conversation and you naturally put your guard up. Even once you've been beaten there's residual anxiety, fear, and uncertainty. When exposed to manufactured consensus, you arrive at a conclusion feeling like it's your own. Your cognitive autonomy is undermined while your guard is down.

In the real world it's just one echo chamber – the misguided group of friends. You conform to the group, not the idea. Our young skeptic has been convinced to go subway surfing, not that subway surfing is safe. Technologically, you're exposed to different echo chambers – different comments sections – echoing the same ideas. You conform to the idea, not the echo chamber.

These differences result in consequences that can be severe. Consider the comments section of a Clavicular video. A young man arrives, nursing suspicion that he is not good enough and as a result women have written him off. Clavicular claims to offer a solution to his pain, telling him to harden himself,isolate himself, and view women as an adversary. In the comments he finds hundreds of men who purport to have benefited from subscribing to Clavicular's worldview.

Yes, the content itself and the social media algorithms that amplify it are largely to blame. But, it's not just Clavicular's insidious messaging and content that is overriding people's basic human instincts. There are two contagions working symbiotically – the content itself and the manufactured consensus in the comments section.

If the comments sections passed mature judgement on his life, viewers would be better off. Or, if there were no comments at all, people would be forced to critically evaluate what they just watched. They could take a moment to pause and reach logical conclusions. But, the comments amplify the messaging of the insidious content and affirm it. The subsequent affirmation overpowers one's natural instincts and substitutes the manufactured consensus. That's not to say, without the comments section, that some people or even a majority would not agree with Clavicular and subscribe to his lifestyle. It's that their decision making process is undermined to the point that the freedom of choice is taken away from them.

Some will be sensationally radicalized by Clavicular's videos and the comments sections, but most will not. Rather, the effect quietly corrodes the beautiful subtleties of life. A few weeks ago, while rain poured down, I huddled under a small awning with a date. As we sought refuge from the storm, for a brief moment, we weren't afraid to connect. It was one of the best moments of my life. If you check Clavicular's comments sections, moments like that are worthless. That is what is at stake here.

Our ability to form genuine thoughts and opinions is now downstream from the technological content we consume. Of course, platforms must protect the user experience and work towards a digital environment free of algorithmic persuasion. But as users, we also have to intentionally and honestly evaluate the role manufactured consensus plays in our lives and protect our cognitive autonomy. If not, the same process that made me grin at my phone will continue to cause genuine harm.
